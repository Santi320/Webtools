The Easter Bunny

The Easter Bunny (also called the Easter Rabbit or Easter Hare) is a folkloric figure and symbol of Easter, depicted as a rabbit—sometimes dressed in clothes—bringing Easter eggs. Originating among German Lutherans, the "Easter Hare" originally played the role of a judge, evaluating whether children were good or disobedient in behavior at the start of Eastertide, similar to Santa’s "naughty or nice" list. As part of the legend, the creature carries colored eggs, candy, and sometimes toys to children’s homes. This custom was first mentioned in Georg Franck von Franckenau's De ovis paschalibus ('About Easter eggs') in 1682.

Rabbits and Hares

The hare was a popular motif in medieval church art. Ancient writers like Pliny and Plutarch believed the hare was a hermaphrodite; this led to an association with the Virgin Mary. Hares appear in illuminated manuscripts and Northern European paintings of the Virgin and Christ Child, and sometimes in the three hares motif symbolizing the Trinity.

Eggs

Eggs have symbolized fertility since antiquity and were tied to Christian rebirth early on. During medieval Lent, eggs were forbidden, so children begged for them on the Saturday before the fast; they then celebrated by decorating and eating them.

As a special dish, eggs would be decorated with flowers to bring spring indoors. German Protestants retained the custom of colored eggs, and Eastern Orthodox Christians still dye eggs red for Christ’s blood. The Ukrainian art of pysanky predates Christianity and survives today across Eastern Europe.

The "Osterhase" tradition arrived in the U.S. with 18th-century Pennsylvania Dutch immigrants. Hase means "hare" in German, and good children found colored eggs in the nests they made before Easter.
